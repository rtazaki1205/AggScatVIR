Appendix A: Definitions

Radius definitions

Because of its complex shapes, there are several ways to define the size of an aggregate. We will use two definitions: (i) the volume-equivalent radius \(a_\mathrm{V}\) and (ii) the characteristic radius \(a_\mathrm{c}\). The former and latter definitions are, so-to-speak, measuring the mass and the apparent size of an aggregate, respectively.

The volume-equivalent radius is the radius of a sphere formed by squashing an aggregate while retaining the material volume. Since the volume of the dust particle is the same before and after the squashing process, we have

\[\frac{4}{3}\pi a_\mathrm{V}^3=N \frac{4}{3}\pi a_\mathrm{mon}^3,\]

where \(a_\mathrm{mon}\) is the monomer radius and \(N\) is the number of monomers. We have also assumed all monomers in the aggregate are identical and spherical. From this, we obtain

\[a_\mathrm{V}=a_\mathrm{mon}N^{1/3}.\]

It is obvious however that an apparent size of an aggregate could be much larger than the volume-equivalent radius, particularly when the porosity is high. We thereby introduce the second definition for the aggregate radius: the characteristic radius \(a_\mathrm{c}\) (Mukai et al. 1992, Kozasa et al. 1992):

\[a_\mathrm{c} = \sqrt{\frac{5}{3}}a_\mathrm{g},\]

where \(a_\mathrm{g}\) is the radius of gyration defined by

\[\begin{split}a_\mathrm{g} = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{N} \frac{ \\|\vec{r_i}-\vec{r_\mathrm{M}}\\|^2 }{N}},\end{split}\]

where \(\vec{r_i}\) is the position vector of \(i\)-th monomer, and \(\vec{r_\mathrm{M}}\) is the position vector of the center of mass of the aggregate. The radius of gyration therefore represents a standard deviation of the distance of monomers with respect to the center of mass.

Fractal dimension, prefactor, and porosity

By using the above two radius definitions, we define the porosity of an aggregate by

\[\mathcal{P} = 1 - \left(\frac{a_\mathrm{V}}{a_\mathrm{c}}\right)^3.\]

Since both \(a_\mathrm{c}\) and \(a_\mathrm{V}\) are proportional to the monomer radius, the porosity is independent of the monomer radius.

It is also useful to introduce the fractal dimension \(D_\mathrm{f}\) defined as follows:

\[N = k_\mathrm{f}\left(\frac{a_\mathrm{g}}{a_\mathrm{mon}}\right)^{D_\mathrm{f}},\]

where \(k_\mathrm{f}\) is the fractal prefactor.

The fractal dimension is a quantity that characterizes the geometric shape of an aggregate. For example, an aggregate with a fractal dimension of 3 has monomers distributed in such a way that the aggregate’s mass grows with the cube of its radius of gyration, similar to an elementary scaling for a single spherical particle. In this case, the aggregate tends to have a compact structure. A fractal dimension may also be less than 3, with lower values corresponding to more open aggregate structures. For example, an aggregate consisting of monomers lined up in a straight chain would have a volume proportional to its radius, resulting in a fractal dimension of 1.

The fractal prefactor \(k_\mathrm{f}\) may be regarded as related to a packing fraction in \(D_\mathrm{f}\) dimensional space. Its meaning becomes clear if we consider the case of \(D_\mathrm{f}=3\). In this case, the above fractal scaling law gives

\[\left(\frac{a_\mathrm{V}}{a_\mathrm{c}}\right)^3=\left(\frac{3}{5}\right)^{3/2}k_\mathrm{f}\approx0.465 k_\mathrm{f} ~~(D_\mathrm{f}=3)\]

By substituting this expression into the definition of porosity, we obtain

\[\mathcal{P}=1-0.465 k_\mathrm{f}~~(D_\mathrm{f}=3)\]

Therefore, the fractal prefactor is related to the porosity of an aggregate.

Definitions of scattering properties

Notations

The definition of the optical properties written in a file dustkapscatmat_XXX.inp follows the definitions of the ones used in RADMC-3D (see also the manual of RADMC-3D).

Suppose we observe scattered light from a collection of identical dust particles and the dust particles are randomly orientated with equal probability, then the scattering matrix, which relates the incident and scattered Stokes parameters, will have 6 independent elements:

\[\begin{split}\begin{pmatrix} I_\mathrm{sca} \\ Q_\mathrm{sca} \\ U_\mathrm{sca} \\ V_\mathrm{sca} \\ \end{pmatrix} = \frac{m_\mathrm{dust}}{d^2} \begin{pmatrix} Z_{11} & Z_{12} & 0 & 0 \\ Z_{12} & Z_{22} & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & Z_{33} & Z_{34} \\ 0 & 0 & -Z_{34} & Z_{44} \\ \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} I_\mathrm{inc} \\ Q_\mathrm{inc} \\ U_\mathrm{inc} \\ V_\mathrm{inc} \\ \end{pmatrix},\end{split}\]

where \(d\) is the distance from a dust particle to the observer, \(m_\mathrm{dust}\) is the mass of a dust particle (\(\mathrm{g}\)), \(Z_{ij}\) is scattering matrix elements. If each dust particle is not spherically symmetric, we have \(Z_{11}\neq Z_{22}\) and \(Z_{33}\neq Z_{44}\).

The scattering matrix elements \(Z_{ij}\) (\(\mathrm{cm}^2/\mathrm{g}/\mathrm{str}\)) are related to the traditional notation via:

\[Z_{ij}=\frac{S_{ij}^\mathrm{BohrenHuffman}}{k^2m_\mathrm{dust}},\]

where \(k\) is the wave number (\(\mathrm{cm}^{-1}\)), and \(S_{ij}\) are the scattering matrix elements defined by Bohren & Huffman (1983). Note that RADMC-3D adopts the IAU 1974 definition for the Stokes vectors, and it is different from what Bohren & Huffman’s book follows. As a result, we need a minus sign for the \(Z_{13}\), \(Z_{14}\), \(Z_{23}\), \(Z_{24}\), \(Z_{31}\), \(Z_{41}\), \(Z_{32}\), \(Z_{42}\) elements because of the opposite \(U\) and \(V\) sign conventions (But, these elements are zero for our cases).

The integration of \(Z_{11}\) over all solid angles gives the scattering opacity (\(\mathrm{cm}^2/\mathrm{g}\))

\[\kappa_\mathrm{sca}(\lambda)=\int_{4\pi} Z_{11}(\theta,\lambda)d\Omega.\]

The definition of the phase function is the same as Bohren & Huffman’s one (see Eq. 13.3 in Chapter 13):

\[p(\theta)\equiv\frac{1}{C_\mathrm{sca}}\frac{dC_\mathrm{sca}}{d\Omega}=\frac{1}{C_\mathrm{sca}/m_\mathrm{dust}}\frac{S_{11}}{k^2m_\mathrm{dust}}=\frac{Z_{11}(\theta)}{\kappa_\mathrm{sca}},\]

where \(C_\mathrm{sca}\) is the scattering cross section, and \(dC_\mathrm{sca}/d\Omega\) is the differential scattering cross section. The phase function is therefore normalized such that \(\int_{4\pi} p(\theta)d\Omega=1\). The asymmetry parameter is defined by (p. 72, Section 3.4 in Bohren & Huffman’s book):

\[g \equiv \int_{4\pi} p(\theta) \cos\theta d\Omega=\frac{2\pi}{\kappa_\mathrm{sca}}\int_{-1}^{+1} Z_{11}(\mu)\mu d\mu,\]

where \(\mu=\cos\theta\); \(\theta\) is the scattering angle (\(\theta=0^\circ\) is the forward scattering direction). \(g\) is a non-dimensional quantity.

Size distribution averaging

This package provides the optical properties averaged over a particle-size distribution. The size distribution is assumed to obey a power-law function:

\[n(a_\mathrm{V})da_\mathrm{V}\propto a_\mathrm{V}^{-q}da_\mathrm{V}~(a_\mathrm{min}\le a_\mathrm{V}\le a_\mathrm{max}),\]

where \(a_\mathrm{V}\) is the volume-equivalent radius of a dust particle, and \(n(a_\mathrm{V})da_\mathrm{V}\) is the number density of particles within [\(a_\mathrm{V},a_\mathrm{V}+da_\mathrm{V}\)]. By default, we adopt \(q=3.5\).

The average scattering matrix elements for a collection of various particle sizes can be expressed as the following integration:

\[Z_{ij,\mathrm{ave}}(\theta)=\frac{\int_{a_\mathrm{min}}^{a_\mathrm{max}}a_\mathrm{V}^{3-q}Z_{ij}(a_\mathrm{V},\theta) da_\mathrm{V}}{\int_{a_\mathrm{min}}^{a_\mathrm{max}} a_\mathrm{V}^{3-q}da_\mathrm{V}},\]

where \(Z_{ij,\mathrm{ave}}\) are the scattering matrix elements averaged over the size distribution (Remember that \(Z_{ij}\) are quantities per unit mass). Similarly, the distribution-averaged absorption opacity is calculated by

\[\kappa_\mathrm{abs,ave} = \frac{\int_{a_\mathrm{min}}^{a_\mathrm{max}}a_\mathrm{V}^{3-q}\kappa_\mathrm{abs}(a_\mathrm{V}) da_\mathrm{V}}{\int_{a_\mathrm{min}}^{a_\mathrm{max}} a_\mathrm{V}^{3-q}da_\mathrm{V}}.\]

The averaged scattering opacity and asymmetry parameter are calculated from the averaged value of \(Z_{11,\mathrm{ave}}\):

\[\begin{split}\kappa_\mathrm{sca,ave}=2\pi \int_{-1}^{+1} Z_{11,\mathrm{ave}}(\mu)d\mu,\\ g_\mathrm{ave}=\frac{2\pi}{\kappa_\mathrm{sca,ave}}\int_{-1}^{+1} Z_{11,\mathrm{ave}}(\mu)\mu d\mu.\end{split}\]
